01 / National Trend

Hospitalisations have been rising — with two sharp breaks in the data

The national count grew from 34,033 in 2011 to a peak of 39,866 in 2019 before dipping during COVID-19 in 2020. Note: breaks in 2012 (Victoria) and 2017 (NSW) reflect changes in hospital admission policies, not genuine decreases in crashes.

⚠ 2012: Victoria changed ED admission policy — counts dropped ~5.6% nationally
⚠ 2017: NSW changed ED admission policy — further reduction in counts
02 / State & Territory

Victoria and Queensland surged while NSW declined

Raw counts favour populous states, but the trends reveal different stories. Victoria climbed from 9,326 to 11,130; Queensland nearly doubled from 6,322 to 10,447. Use the toggle to compare states. Note: raw counts are not population-adjusted — the NT's smaller numbers reflect smaller population, not safer roads.

03 / Demographics

Males, middle-aged adults, and motorcyclists bear the greatest burden

In every year on record, males outnumbered females by roughly 2:1. The 40–64 age group consistently accounts for the highest absolute count. Among road users, car drivers top the list — but motorcyclists are vastly overrepresented relative to their share of road use.

04 / First Nations People

A widening and alarming disparity

First Nations hospitalisations grew from 1,167 in 2011 to 2,290 in 2020 — a ~96% increase, while non-Indigenous counts remained relatively stable. First Nations people make up approximately 3.2% of Australia's population yet accounted for ~5.6% of all road crash hospitalisations in 2021. This disparity demands urgent policy attention.
